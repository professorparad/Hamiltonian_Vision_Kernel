\begin{abstract}
We study whether the quantum components of a physics-informed hybrid quantum--classical image autoencoder (the Hamiltonian Vision Kernel, HVK1D/HVK2D --- MPS features $\to$ variational quantum circuit $\to$ learnable Heisenberg energy $\to$ classical decoder) contribute measurably to performance, using a resource-matched, freeze-based, multi-seed ablation protocol. On the \textit{Monalisa} single-image benchmark, matched 240-step ablations over five seeds show that freezing the classical decoder collapses reconstruction to $10.93\pm0.00$~dB while freezing the trained quantum circuit does not hurt it ($33.26\pm0.10$~dB, above the $32.97\pm0.22$~dB trained-VQC baseline); no-entanglement and no-MPS variants are statistically tied with baseline, while no-energy-loss and small classical replacements significantly exceed it. On real held-out CIFAR-10 pixel reconstruction --- five random splits, 20 held-out reconstructions, strict same-width classical controls --- local-observable and raw-linear controls score $18.80\pm1.42$~dB versus HVK2D's $18.12\pm1.54$~dB (paired Wilcoxon $p=9.5\times10^{-6}$, $n=20$), and the same direction holds across five further real datasets (MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, PneumoniaMNIST). We report this negative result as the paper's central finding rather than a limitation appended after the fact. We further document, as a worked methodological lesson, a benchmark of our own that reported a near-perfect fit ($R^2\approx1$, a $120$~dB PSNR that we show is a metric-floor artifact) for the entangling observable channel, trace it to target--feature leakage in which the regression target was constructed from the same distant-patch product quantities handed to the model as input features, and distill the diagnostic that caught it. Within this negative-result frame, two narrower positive results survive scrutiny: a restricted pair-correlation diagnostic --- deliberately constructed, and free of the leakage above --- shows entangling pair observables are representationally necessary to fit targets built from distant-patch products (mean $R^2=0.974$ versus $R^2\leq0.02$ for non-entangling controls, five seeds); and a $D_4$-pooled observable-grid variant is equivariant to the symmetries of the square by construction, verified numerically to $9.6\times10^{-17}$ mean error against $0.74$--$0.84$ for unpooled variants. Our contribution is therefore the evaluation protocol itself --- a resource-matched ablation and leakage-audited statistical validation suite --- together with a clean, reproducible negative result on natural-image reconstruction, a transferable leakage diagnostic, and a narrowly scoped, honestly bounded pair of positive results (entanglement-necessity and provable symmetry) that do not extrapolate into a natural-image or hardware quantum-advantage claim.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Hybrid quantum--classical models for vision tasks are increasingly proposed as a route to representations with favorable inductive bias, exploiting entanglement, physically motivated regularizers, or tensor-network structure that classical convolutional pipelines do not natively expose \cite{Cerezo2021, Tilly2022, Preskill2018}. The variational quantum eigensolver and its descendants exploit the local decomposability of physically relevant Hamiltonians to construct shallow, problem-aware circuits \cite{Cerezo2021, Tilly2022, Bauer2020}, and quantum image processing provides encodings that place pixel information directly into qubit registers \cite{Chakraborty2018, Zhou2017, Yao2017}. Tensor-network methods, in parallel, have proven effective as compressed representations of structured classical data, including images \cite{Cirac2021, Latorre2005, Han2018, Stoudenmire2016, Fei2021, Guala2023, Araz2022}.

The methodological gap is not architectural but evaluative. Hybrid quantum vision models routinely report an advantage over classical baselines without isolating \emph{which component} of the pipeline is responsible, without matching the classical control's parameter budget to the quantum model's, and without auditing whether the reported advantage survives a same-set-versus-held-out distinction or a check for target--feature leakage. Component-wise, resource-matched, leakage-audited evaluation of hybrid quantum--classical vision models is rare; most reported comparisons are of a fully trained hybrid model against an under-matched or task-mismatched classical baseline \cite{Haga2024, Zhai2025, Nau2023, Liu2022}. This makes it difficult to tell whether a reported gain reflects the quantum component, the classical decoder around it, or an evaluation artifact.

In this paper we introduce no new architecture. Instead we take an existing physics-informed hybrid quantum--classical image autoencoder, the Hamiltonian Vision Kernel (HVK1D/HVK2D), and subject it to a controlled ablation and statistical validation protocol designed to answer one question precisely: does any quantum component of this pipeline do measurable work on real, held-out images? We report the answer as a primary finding, not a caveat, and we treat the protocol itself --- not the architecture --- as the paper's central contribution.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A resource-matched, multi-seed, freeze-based ablation protocol} that isolates the marginal value of each quantum component (the trained variational circuit, entangling gates, the Heisenberg energy term, and the MPS feature encoder) against classical controls matched in feature width and readout parameter count.
    \item \textbf{A clean negative result on held-out natural images.} Under this protocol, no quantum component of HVK is load-bearing on held-out CIFAR-10 or five further real image datasets; matched classical controls tie or win, with the CIFAR-10 comparison statistically significant under a paired Wilcoxon test.
    \item \textbf{A leakage-detection diagnostic}, demonstrated on a circular benchmark of our own that it invalidates --- a transferable cautionary methodology for hybrid quantum machine learning evaluation.
    \item \textbf{A provable $D_4$ symmetry extension and a scoped entanglement-necessity result.} A group-pooled HVK2D observable map is equivariant to the square dihedral group by construction (verified to floating-point precision), and a restricted, leakage-free diagnostic shows entangling pair observables are representationally necessary when a target explicitly depends on distant-patch products --- narrow, honestly bounded results that do not extrapolate to the negative reconstruction finding above.
\end{itemize}

\section{Hamiltonian Vision Kernel: Architecture (Compact)}
\label{sec:architecture}
HVK treats each spatial patch of an input image as a quantum many-body state on a finite spin-1/2 chain, encodes that state as a matrix product state (MPS), measures a fixed set of Pauli observables from a variational quantum circuit (VQC) applied to that state, and decodes the observables back into pixels with a classical multi-layer perceptron (MLP). A learnable Heisenberg energy term regularizes training. The full pipeline is:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Image} &\rightarrow \{\text{Patches}\} \rightarrow \text{MPS}\\
&\rightarrow \text{46-D Feature} \rightarrow \text{6-D Vector}\\
&\rightarrow \text{27-D Latent} \rightarrow \text{Decoder}\\
&\rightarrow \text{Reconstruction}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
An input image is resized to $256\times256$ and partitioned into sixteen non-overlapping $64\times64$ patches, each carrying a normalized grid coordinate. Each patch is $\ell_2$-normalized, reshaped into a 12-site spin state $\lvert\psi_n\rangle \in (\mathbb{C}^2)^{\otimes 12}$, and compressed via sequential SVD into an MPS of bond dimension $\chi=4$. Local Pauli expectations, nearest-neighbor $ZZ$ correlations, and bipartite entanglement entropies are read off the MPS to form a 46-dimensional classical feature vector, linearly compressed to 6 dimensions and injected into a 6-qubit VQC via $R_Y$ angle embedding, $R_Z$ positional modulation (from a 2D Fourier encoding of the patch coordinate, \cite{Xu2021}), and two strongly entangling layers of single-qubit rotations and CNOT rings, initialized to avoid barren plateaus \cite{Cerezo2021, McClean2018}. The circuit outputs 27 real Pauli expectations $\Phi_n\in[-1,1]^{27}$ (local $X,Z$; nearest-neighbor $ZZ$, $XX$, $YY$), in the spirit of quantum-enhanced feature maps that use measured expectation values, rather than the raw state, as input to a classical layer \cite{Havlicek2019}. A classical MLP decoder with a mirrored expanding-channel structure ($4\to8\to16\to3$) maps the standardized observable vector, together with the positional encoding, back to pixels. Exact gate sequences, the full Heisenberg Hamiltonian and energy-loss definitions, and the $D_4$-pooling construction's group-theoretic motivation are given in Appendix~\ref{app:circuits}; we flag here, because it foreshadows Section~\ref{sec:results}, that the classical decoder is the only component with unrestricted trainable capacity in this pipeline --- the VQC has 2 entangling layers over 6 qubits, and the Heisenberg couplings are 15 scalar parameters.

Table~\ref{tab:variants} summarizes the tested variants. HVK1D uses a linear 6-qubit chain (5 nearest-neighbor bonds, 27-D observable vector); HVK2D arranges the same 6 qubits on a $2\times3$ lattice matching patch-boundary adjacency (19-D latent signature); a symmetric HVK1D variant adds a $\mathrm{U}(1)$-preserving coupling constraint; and a $D_4$-pooled HVK2D variant (Section~\ref{sec:symmetry}) group-averages the observable grid over the eight symmetries of the square, $\Phi_{D_4}(x)=\frac{1}{8}\sum_{g\in D_4}P_g^{-1}\Phi(gx)$, which is equivariant by construction. An IBM Cloud circuit probe (Appendix~\ref{app:hardware}) confirms both HVK1D and HVK2D compile to depth-18, 6-qubit circuits on a Heron-class basis --- a feasibility check, not a hardware reconstruction result.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}
\subsection{Ablation and Control Design}
We isolate the marginal contribution of each component via targeted freezes and replacements: \emph{freeze-classical} trains only the quantum circuit and Hamiltonian couplings while holding the classical decoder/projection layers fixed at initialization; \emph{freeze-quantum} does the reverse; \emph{no-entanglement} removes the CNOT rings from the VQC; \emph{no-energy-loss} removes the Heisenberg regularizer; \emph{classical-replacement} and \emph{classical-matched} substitute the entire VQC with a classical nonlinearity (a tanh map, in the matched case rank-limited to the same parameter count as the quantum circuit it replaces). Critically, every classical control in the held-out CIFAR-10 layer is \emph{resource-matched}: identical feature width (32-D) and identical linear readout parameter count (2112), so that any residual gap reflects feature structure rather than decoder capacity (Table~\ref{tab:resource_capacity}).

\subsection{Statistical Protocol}
The core component ablations in Table~\ref{tab:component_ablation} are now run at matched convergence: every variant uses the same 240-step budget, learning rate, architecture size, and five random seeds on the \textit{Monalisa} benchmark. We report mean $\pm$ standard deviation and mark any gap smaller than one pooled baseline/control PSNR standard deviation as not significant. Remaining capacity sweeps in Table~\ref{tab:capacity_ablation} are single-seed directional diagnostics. The held-out CIFAR-10 validation layer and the restricted pair-correlation diagnostic (Sections~\ref{sec:results}--\ref{sec:symmetry}) are also multi-seed: five random seeds for the restricted diagnostic, and five random image splits (20 held-out reconstructions total) for real CIFAR-10, each using a shared linear readout trained on a disjoint image subset. Paired statistics (bootstrap 95\% CI, Wilcoxon signed-rank) are computed image-by-image within each split.

\subsection{Leakage Auditing}
Because several of our diagnostics are deliberately constructed so that a target depends on features only some models receive, we audit every such diagnostic for a specific failure mode: a regression target that is a (near-)linear function of a quantity handed verbatim to only one candidate model, which produces an apparent representational advantage that is actually copying rather than learning. Section~\ref{sec:leakage} documents a diagnostic of our own that failed this audit, and states the check as a general, reusable rule.

\subsection{Datasets and Metrics}
We use three settings, in increasing order of evidentiary weight for a generalization claim. \textit{Single-image} (\textit{Monalisa}, $256\times256$, 16 patches of $64\times64$) tests per-image optimization only. \textit{CIFAR-10 same-set} (five native $32\times32$ images) is a same-set reconstruction fit, useful for capacity/positioning comparisons but not a generalization test --- the MLP baseline's same-set PSNR exceeds $100$~dB in the best case, a memorization red flag rather than evidence of representation quality. \textit{Held-out CIFAR-10} (five random splits, disjoint train/held-out images, 20 total held-out reconstructions) and a six-dataset multi-dataset suite (CIFAR-10, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, PneumoniaMNIST, plus a Wisconsin Breast Cancer tabular cross-domain check) are the settings that test a \emph{learned representation} rather than per-image memorization, and are therefore the basis for this paper's central claim. All settings report MSE and PSNR $=20\log_{10}(1/\sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}})$; image settings additionally report SSIM.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{The Decoder Is the Model: Matched Multi-Seed Freeze Isolation}
Table~\ref{tab:component_ablation} and Figure~\ref{fig:component_ablation_recons} give the matched-convergence freeze-isolation result on \textit{Monalisa}: nine variants, five seeds each, all trained for 240 steps. Freezing the classical decoder and projection layers while training only the quantum circuit and Hamiltonian couplings (\emph{freeze-classical}) collapses reconstruction to $10.93\pm0.00$~dB PSNR ($\mathrm{SSIM}=0.0220\pm0.0002$) --- close to noise. Freezing the VQC and Hamiltonian couplings while training only the classical decoder (\emph{freeze-quantum}) reaches $33.26\pm0.10$~dB, above the trained-VQC baseline of $32.97\pm0.22$~dB. A classical tanh replacement of the entire VQC reaches $33.50\pm0.00$~dB, a parameter-matched classical map reaches $33.37\pm0.08$~dB, and removing the signed energy regularizer reaches $33.40\pm0.06$~dB. No-MPS and no-entanglement variants are statistically indistinguishable from baseline under the one-pooled-standard-deviation rule. The shuffle-control contradiction was separately resolved: the true five-permutation observable-shuffle drop is only $0.301\pm0.054$~dB, not the stale $12.5$~dB value from an older write-up. Thus the robust conclusion is stronger than the original single-seed result: the trainable classical decoder/projection stack is load-bearing, while trained quantum parameters, entangling gates, MPS features, and the signed Hamiltonian regularizer are not isolated as necessary for this reconstruction task.

\subsection{Every Quantum Ablation Ties or Wins on Held-Out Real Images}
\label{sec:holdout_headline}
This is the paper's headline negative result, and the one setting designed from the outset to test a learned representation rather than per-image memorization. Table~\ref{tab:hvk_real_cifar} reports held-out CIFAR-10 reconstruction (five random splits, 20 held-out reconstructions, all controls resource-matched per Table~\ref{tab:resource_capacity}), and Table~\ref{tab:hvk_real_cifar_stats} the paired statistics against the HVK2D real-CIFAR map. Local-observables-only and raw-linear-classical controls (identical by construction here) reach $18.80\pm1.42$~dB, ZZ-only $18.56\pm1.49$~dB, no-entanglement $18.46\pm1.32$~dB, shuffled-pair $18.31\pm1.25$~dB, and quadratic-classical $18.33\pm1.65$~dB --- all statistically indistinguishable from, or better than, the trained HVK2D entangling map's $18.12\pm1.54$~dB. The gap against the strongest control (raw-linear) is small in absolute terms ($0.68$~dB) but consistent across all 20 paired held-out images (Wilcoxon $p=9.5\times10^{-6}$); HVK2D remains far above a random-VQC control ($11.15\pm1.19$~dB), so the observable channel is not meaningless, but no quantum ablation --- trained, frozen, or entanglement-stripped --- beats a plain linear readout from raw or local pixel statistics.

\subsection{Order-Parameter Tracking and Critical Epochs}
\label{sec:order_parameter_tracking}
In addition to pixel metrics, we track a spin-order diagnostic during training to ask whether the observable channel undergoes a coherent representational transition. Let $z_{n,i}^{(e)}=\langle Z_i\rangle$ and $x_{n,i}^{(e)}=\langle X_i\rangle$ denote the measured local Pauli expectations for patch $n$ and qubit/site $i$ at epoch $e$, with $N$ patches and $q$ measured sites. We define the longitudinal order parameter, absolute longitudinal order, and transverse order as
\begin{align}
m_Z(e) &= \frac{1}{Nq}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{q} z_{n,i}^{(e)},\\
|m_Z|(e) &= \frac{1}{Nq}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{q} \left|z_{n,i}^{(e)}\right|,\\
m_X(e) &= \frac{1}{Nq}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=1}^{q} x_{n,i}^{(e)}.
\end{align}
The discrete susceptibility is the epoch-to-epoch change in longitudinal order,
\begin{equation}
\chi_m(e)=\left|m_Z(e)-m_Z(e-1)\right|,\qquad \chi_m(0)=0,
\end{equation}
and the critical epoch is estimated by
\begin{equation}
e_c=\arg\max_e \chi_m(e).
\end{equation}
This is not a thermodynamic phase transition in the infinite-system sense: the system is a finite six-qubit observable map coupled to a classical decoder. We use ``critical epoch'' in the finite-size learning-dynamics sense, as the training point where the measured latent order changes most sharply.

Figure~\ref{fig:order_parameter_phase_transition} shows the clean order-parameter evidence after removing the less relevant energy panel from the diagnostic plots. The behavior is visually strong: \textit{Monalisa} HVK2D develops a sharp longitudinal ordering ramp with $e_c=138$; a CIFAR cat example transitions later, at $e_c=152$; and a CIFAR frog example transitions earlier, at $e_c=75$, with a sign reversal in $m_Z$ while $|m_Z|$ increases. These trajectories show that the observable channel is not random noise: the measured Pauli latents reorganize during training in a reproducible, image-dependent manner. The important scope boundary is that this is an interpretability and optimization diagnostic, not evidence that the quantum component improves held-out reconstruction; Section~\ref{sec:holdout_headline} shows that matched local/raw controls still tie or win on real held-out images.

\section{A Circular Benchmark, and How to Catch It}
\label{sec:leakage}
Before the held-out result in Section~\ref{sec:holdout_headline} became this paper's central claim, we had a different one: a ``CIFAR-derived nonlocal patch-correlation diagnostic'' in which the trained HVK2D entangling observable map reached mean $R^2=1.0000$ and a reported PSNR of $120.00$~dB, while every non-entangling control (raw-linear, one-sided patch, no-entanglement, random-feature, random VQC) scored at or below $R^2\approx0.24$. Taken at face value this looked like the strongest evidence in the paper for a genuine, task-general entanglement advantage. It was not, and we document the failure here because catching it required a specific, reusable check that we believe is broadly useful for this literature.

\subsection{What went wrong}
The diagnostic's regression target was constructed, for a pair of distant CIFAR patches with feature vectors \texttt{left} and \texttt{right}, as six explicit product terms:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
y = \bigl(&\mathrm{left}_0\mathrm{right}_0,\ \mathrm{left}_1\mathrm{right}_1,\ \mathrm{left}_4\mathrm{right}_5,\ \mathrm{left}_7\mathrm{right}_8,\\
&(\mathrm{left}_2{-}\mathrm{left}_3)(\mathrm{right}_4{-}\mathrm{right}_5),\\
&\sin\!\bigl(\pi(\mathrm{left}_0{+}\mathrm{right}_0)\bigr)(\mathrm{left}_7{+}\mathrm{right}_8)\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The ``entangling'' feature map handed to the candidate model computed the \emph{identical six formulas} as part of its own feature construction, concatenated them with the raw left/right/delta features, and truncated the result to a fixed width; the six target-matching columns survived the truncation intact. A ridge regression with near-zero regularization ($10^{-6}$) then only had to select and rescale those six columns to reach machine-precision fit --- it was not learning a representation of nonlocal structure, it was copying the answer key that had been concatenated onto its own input. The reported $120.00$~dB PSNR compounds this: our PSNR computation clamps MSE to a floor of $10^{-12}$ before taking $20\log_{10}(1/\sqrt{\mathrm{MSE}})$, and $20\log_{10}(10^{6})=120$ exactly --- the number is an artifact of that floor, not a measurement, since the true MSE ($4.4\times10^{-15}$) is far below it. The non-entangling controls failed not because they lacked a quantum mechanism, but because they were never given the leaked columns: a purely classical control that \emph{was} given five of the same six product formulas reached $R^2=0.79$ (short of $1.0$ only because it lacked the $\sin(\cdot)$ term), which is the clearest evidence that the effect tracked column presence, not entanglement.

\subsection{The diagnostic that caught it, and a reusable rule}
The check that exposed this is simple and, in hindsight, should be run on every diagnostic where a target is deliberately constructed to align with a candidate feature map: \emph{verify that no model receives, as a precomputed input feature, a quantity from which the regression or classification target is linearly (or near-linearly) recoverable.} Concretely, for a target $y$ and a candidate feature block $x$, check whether a linear or near-linear map from $x$ to $y$ achieves near-zero residual on features the target-construction code itself used to build $y$ --- if so, the feature block and the target share provenance and the diagnostic is circular regardless of which model architecture receives the leaked block. In our case this meant tracing the target-construction function and every feature-construction function line-by-line and checking for shared closed-form subexpressions, not just shared input columns after preprocessing. We found and flagged this internally before this manuscript's submission; the diagnostic and its result are withdrawn from every quantitative claim in this paper (they do not appear in Sections~\ref{sec:results} or~\ref{sec:symmetry}), and we report the post-mortem here, rather than silently deleting it, because the same construction pattern --- building a favorable target from a model's own input features --- is an easy mistake to repeat with a different variable naming scheme, and is not specific to quantum architectures. Appendix~\ref{app:leakage} gives the full target- and feature-construction code for reproducibility.

\section{A Restricted Entanglement-Necessity Result and a Provable Symmetry Extension}
\label{sec:symmetry}
Section~\ref{sec:leakage}'s failure does not mean every diagnostic favorable to entangling observables is invalid; it means each one must be independently checked for the specific leakage pattern above. The two results in this section were audited against that check and do not share the withdrawn diagnostic's construction.

\subsection{Restricted pair-correlation diagnostic}
This diagnostic is deliberately constructed to reward pair-observable structure --- unlike Section~\ref{sec:leakage}'s withdrawn benchmark, the target here is a fixed synthetic function of \emph{simulated} patch features not concatenated into any candidate's own input block, and every control receives the same raw inputs as HVK2D. Table~\ref{tab:hvk_pair_diagnostic} and Figure~\ref{fig:hvk_pair_diagnostic} report the result across five seeds: HVK2D's entangling observable channel reaches mean $R^2=0.9735$ ($32.77$~dB PSNR), a $17.74$~dB improvement over a no-entanglement control ($R^2=0.019$) and $18.52$~dB over random-VQC latents ($R^2=-0.173$); freezing the quantum circuit (features fixed, only readout trained) still reaches $R^2=0.853$, while a parameter-matched classical control ($R^2=-0.030$) and a frozen-classical control ($R^2=-0.903$) fail outright. \subsection{Provable $D_4$ symmetry extension}
\label{sec:d4}
Independently of any entanglement claim, a $D_4$-pooled HVK2D observable-grid map is equivariant to the eight symmetries of the square \emph{by construction}: $\Phi_{D_4}(x)=\frac{1}{8}\sum_{g\in D_4}P_g^{-1}\Phi(gx)$ averages the unpooled map over the group orbit, so $\Phi_{D_4}(hx)=P_h\Phi_{D_4}(x)$ for every $h\in D_4$ up to floating-point error. We verify this numerically over 7000 patch transforms of cached CIFAR images (Table~\ref{tab:d4_equivariance}, Figure~\ref{fig:d4_equivariance}): the pooled map's equivariance error is $9.57\times10^{-17}\pm1.26\times10^{-17}$ (floating-point noise), while the original unpooled positional map is not measurably more symmetric than a local/raw control ($0.815\pm0.129$ vs.\ $0.837\pm0.131$) --- confirming the unpooled map is only $D_4$-\emph{motivated} by the patch grid's geometry, not empirically equivariant, and that only the explicit pooling construction delivers the property. This is a feature-grid-level construction and numerical diagnostic: it has not been integrated into a retrained end-to-end reconstruction pipeline, so it is a symmetry guarantee for the pooled observable map, not a reconstruction-quality claim, and it does not bear on Section~\ref{sec:holdout_headline}'s negative result.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Why the Physics-Informed Regularizer Was Inert}
Table~\ref{tab:hamiltonian_controls} shows the Hamiltonian energy term is, if anything, a mild drag on reconstruction quality in the present system. The legacy objective adds a signed mean energy to the reconstruction loss; because learned energy can go negative, the optimizer can lower total loss by driving energy down without improving reconstruction, and removing the term improves PSNR from $32.24$ to $33.30$~dB. A contrastive variant --- assigning lower energy to correct feature--position pairs than to shuffled pairs --- improves over the signed-energy baseline ($32.84$~dB) but remains below its own no-energy-loss counterpart ($33.33$~dB), and ZZ-only observables ($32.88$~dB) show nearest-neighbor correlations are largely sufficient on their own. We read this as a case where the reconstruction loss already dominates the optimization signal at this model scale and dataset size: a physically motivated regularizer has room to help when it resolves an otherwise underdetermined objective, and here the pixel-fidelity loss alone is sufficiently informative that the extra term mostly adds noise to the gradient. This does not mean Hamiltonian regularization is never useful --- only that its value is conditional on the reconstruction loss being under-constrained, a condition this patch-level pixel task does not meet.

\subsection{Dequantization Context}
\label{sec:dequant}
The tie-with-classical outcome in Section~\ref{sec:holdout_headline} is consistent with a broader pattern in quantum machine learning: whether a quantum feature map or kernel offers any advantage over a classical model is a property of the specific data and target, not a fixed property of the model class \cite{Huang2021PowerData}, and dequantization results have repeatedly shown that apparent quantum advantages on structured, low-entanglement classical data can be matched by classical algorithms once the right classical feature is identified \cite{Havlicek2019}. Natural image patches, at the resolution and patch size used here, are exactly this kind of low-entanglement, locally structured data --- a regime in which a linear or shallow classical readout over raw or local pixel statistics is already close to sufficient, leaving little room for an entangling feature map to add information the pixel statistics did not already carry. The restricted diagnostic in Section~\ref{sec:symmetry}.A is the contrasting case: its target is constructed so that the relevant structure is a genuine distant-element product that raw or local features cannot represent under a fixed linear readout, and that is precisely the regime in which the entangling channel earns its advantage. The two results together, rather than being in tension, delineate the boundary: entanglement is necessary when the task's structure demands nonlocal products a linear/local map cannot express, and unnecessary when it does not, and ordinary natural-image reconstruction at this scale falls on the second side of that line.

\subsection{Design and Evaluation Guidelines}
We distill the protocol in Section~\ref{sec:protocol} and the failure in Section~\ref{sec:leakage} into concrete guidance for evaluating hybrid quantum--classical vision models: (1) \emph{resource-match} every classical control to the quantum model's feature width and readout parameter count, not just its input dimensionality; (2) \emph{freeze-isolate} each trainable component (quantum circuit, classical decoder, physics-informed regularizer) independently before crediting any one of them with an observed gain; (3) \emph{audit every favorable diagnostic for target--feature leakage} by tracing target-construction and feature-construction code for shared subexpressions, not just shared preprocessing; (4) \emph{report held-out, not same-set, performance} as the primary evidence for a representation-learning claim, and label same-set numbers explicitly as capacity diagnostics; (5) \emph{report multiple seeds with error bars} on any comparison intended to support a quantitative claim, and mark single-seed sweeps as directional. We offer these as the paper's principal take-home value, independent of whether they are applied to HVK or any other hybrid architecture.

\subsection{Topology Alignment as an Inductive Bias}
The $+5.98$~dB advantage of 2D HVK over its 1D counterpart on \textit{Monalisa} (Table~\ref{tab:cifar10}; $40.70$ vs.\ $34.72$~dB) is consistent with a topology-alignment hypothesis --- aligning the latent interaction graph with image-patch adjacency can help reconstruction --- but it is a single-image, single-seed observation, not a general principle, since the strongest 1D/2D comparison is documented on one test case rather than a multi-seed dataset. We treat topology alignment as an architectural inductive bias whose contribution has not yet been separated from decoder capacity, optimization budget, and dataset-specific structure.

\subsection{Limitations and Scope}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The original CIFAR-10 same-set benchmark is bounded to a five-image native-resolution subset; the multi-dataset runner uses a larger cached 400-image CIFAR-10 subset before stratified splitting.
    \item CIFAR and \textit{Monalisa} same-set comparisons measure fit capacity, not held-out generalization; the MLP/CNN same-set numbers should not be read against Section~\ref{sec:holdout_headline}.
    \item 2D HVK topology performance data is documented solely for the \textit{Monalisa} test case (Section~\ref{sec:results}.E).
    \item The core component table is multi-seed and matched at 240 steps, but the Hamiltonian and capacity sweeps remain single-seed directional diagnostics; small PSNR/SSIM differences in Tables~\ref{tab:capacity_ablation} and~\ref{tab:hamiltonian_controls} should not be read as statistically resolved.
    \item The circular CIFAR-derived nonlocal diagnostic documented in Section~\ref{sec:leakage} has been withdrawn from every quantitative claim in this paper; the entanglement-necessity claim rests solely on the restricted diagnostic of Section~\ref{sec:symmetry}.A, which was independently audited against the same leakage pattern.
    \item The $D_4$-pooled map (Section~\ref{sec:d4}) is an enforced feature-grid equivariance construction and numerical diagnostic, not yet integrated into a retrained end-to-end reconstruction pipeline.
    \item No hardware execution results are reported; the IBM circuit probe (Appendix~\ref{app:hardware}) describes compiled-circuit feasibility only.
    \item Held-out CIFAR-10 validation is intentionally lightweight (20 held-out reconstructions from five cached splits); a full class-balanced CIFAR-10 test-set protocol remains future work, as does full end-to-end retraining of the architecture per dataset in the multi-dataset suite.
    \item The Wisconsin Breast Cancer check is a tabular cross-domain sanity check, not image-reconstruction evidence, and does not favor the HVK pair map over the raw-linear control.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
We took an existing physics-informed hybrid quantum--classical image autoencoder, HVK1D/HVK2D, and asked a narrower and more answerable question than ``does it work'': which of its components, if any, are load-bearing on real, held-out images? Under a resource-matched, freeze-based, multi-seed ablation protocol, the answer is that the trainable classical decoder is necessary and sufficient, and no quantum component --- trained circuit, entangling gates, or Hamiltonian energy term --- is shown to be. On real held-out CIFAR-10, local-observable and raw-linear controls reach $18.80\pm1.42$~dB against HVK2D's $18.12\pm1.54$~dB (Wilcoxon $p=9.5\times10^{-6}$, $n=20$), and the same direction holds on five further real datasets. We report this as the paper's central, positive methodological contribution rather than a disappointing footnote: a rigorous protocol that resolves the question is more valuable than an unresolved claim of advantage.

The protocol also caught our own mistake. A CIFAR-derived nonlocal diagnostic that had reported a near-perfect fit for the entangling observable channel was traced, on internal audit, to target--feature leakage --- the regression target was built from the same product terms handed to the model as input --- and is withdrawn from every quantitative claim here. We report the post-mortem (Section~\ref{sec:leakage}) as a transferable diagnostic: verify that no candidate model receives, as a precomputed feature, a quantity from which the target is linearly recoverable, by tracing target- and feature-construction code for shared subexpressions rather than trusting that dissimilar preprocessing implies independence.

Two narrower positive results survive this scrutiny. A restricted, leakage-audited pair-correlation diagnostic shows entangling observables are representationally necessary when a target explicitly depends on distant-patch products ($R^2=0.9735$ vs.\ $R^2\leq0.02$ for non-entangling controls, five seeds), and a $D_4$-pooled HVK2D observable map is equivariant to the symmetries of the square by construction, verified to $9.57\times10^{-17}$ mean error against $0.74$--$0.84$ for unpooled variants. Neither result extrapolates to the negative reconstruction finding above: the entanglement-necessity result is scoped to a task deliberately built to require it, and the symmetry result is a feature-grid-level guarantee not yet wired into a trainable reconstruction pipeline.

Future work should extend the resource-matched, leakage-audited protocol used here to other hybrid quantum vision architectures, integrate the $D_4$-pooled map into an end-to-end trainable pipeline, scale the multi-dataset suite beyond lightweight fixed-feature readouts, and search --- under the same protocol --- for tasks where Hamiltonian structure or entanglement remain beneficial after strict resource-matched classical controls, since the dequantization context of Section~\ref{sec:dequant} suggests such tasks exist but ordinary natural-image reconstruction, at the scale tested here, is not one of them.

\section{Exact Circuit Definitions}
\label{app:circuits}
For a one-dimensional chain of $n_{\text{bonds}}$ bonds indexed by $i$ and sites $(i,i+1)$, the anisotropic Heisenberg Hamiltonian is
\begin{equation}
H_{\text{Heis}} = \sum_{i=0}^{n_{\text{bonds}}-1}\Bigl(J_x^{(i)}X_i X_{i+1} + J_y^{(i)}Y_i Y_{i+1} + J_z^{(i)}Z_i Z_{i+1}\Bigr),
\end{equation}
where $X,Y,Z$ are Pauli operators and $J_\alpha^{(i)}$ bond-resolved couplings. In the isotropic limit ($J_x=J_y=J_z$) the model has exact $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ symmetry; for $J_x=J_y\neq J_z$ (XXZ) the $\mathrm{U}(1)$ symmetry generated by $\sum Z_i$ is preserved; for generic couplings (XYZ) this breaks to $\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2$ \cite{Craps2024}. HVK fixes six sites and treats every $J_\alpha^{(i)}$ as a learnable parameter, $J_\alpha^{(i)}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,0.01)$. Given patch $n$'s measured observables, the Heisenberg energy is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{E}_n(\theta,J) = \sum_{i=0}^{4}\sum_{\alpha\in\{x,y,z\}} J_\alpha^{(i)}\langle\sigma_\alpha^{(i)}\sigma_\alpha^{(i+1)}\rangle_n,
\end{equation}
and the patch-averaged training energy loss is $\mathcal{L}_{\text{energy}}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_n \mathcal{E}_n$. The total objective combines pixel fidelity with this regularizer,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{rec}} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{energy}}, \quad
\mathcal{L}_{\text{rec}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\bigl\|D(o_n,e_n)-P_n\bigr\|_F^2,
\end{equation}
with $D$ the classical decoder and $P_n$ the ground-truth patch.

Per-patch coordinates $(r_i,c_j)$ are mapped to an 8-D Fourier positional vector, $e_{4k}=\sin(\omega_k\pi r_i)$, $e_{4k+1}=\cos(\omega_k\pi r_i)$, $e_{4k+2}=\sin(\omega_k\pi c_j)$, $e_{4k+3}=\cos(\omega_k\pi c_j)$ for $k\in\{0,1\}$ \cite{Xu2021}, projected via a learned $W_{\text{pos}}\in\mathbb{R}^{6\times8}$ to $\theta_{\text{position}}=W_{\text{pos}}e\in\mathbb{R}^6$. The 6-qubit circuit applies, in order: angle embedding $U_{\text{enc}}(x_n)=\bigotimes_{i=0}^{5}R_Y(x_n^{(i)})$; positional modulation $U_{\text{pos}}=\bigotimes_{i=0}^{5}R_Z(\theta_{\text{position}}^{(i)})$; and $L=2$ strongly entangling layers $U_{\text{var}}(\theta)=\prod_{l=1}^{L}\bigl[\text{CNOT-Ring}\cdot\bigotimes_{i=0}^{5}\text{Rot}(\phi_i^l,\theta_i^l,\omega_i^l)\bigr]$, giving $|\Psi_n\rangle = U_{\text{var}}U_{\text{pos}}U_{\text{enc}}|0\rangle^{\otimes6}$.

The Heisenberg correlators define three observable sectors $\Xi_{ZZ},\Xi_{XX},\Xi_{YY}$ embedded in $\mathbb{R}^{27}$. Two symmetry reductions act sequentially: the per-qubit positional rotations belong to $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ (a double cover of spatial $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ Bloch rotations) and break qubit-site permutation symmetry down to the Cartan sector; and the $4\times4$ patch grid carries the dihedral group $D_4=\langle r,s\mid r^4=s^2=(sr)^2=e\rangle$, under which observable vectors transform as $o_{g(i,j)}\approx\Phi_g\,o_{i,j}$, decomposing the latent space as $\mathcal{V}_{\text{obs}}\approx m_AA_1\oplus m_BA_2\oplus m_CB_1\oplus m_DB_2\oplus m_EE$. This motivates the reduction $\mathrm{SU}(2)\text{-isotropic}\xrightarrow{\text{positional encoding}}\text{Cartan}\xrightarrow{\text{grid geometry}}D_4$, which the unpooled map only motivates rather than enforces (Section~\ref{sec:d4} gives the pooling construction that does enforce it). Building group structure into a learned observable map mirrors group-equivariant convolutional networks in classical vision \cite{Cohen2016} and analogous symmetry-embedding constructions for variational quantum circuits \cite{Meyer2023}.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:implementation}
Adam optimizer, $\eta=0.003$ (1D) / $\eta=0.004$ (2D); energy regularization $\lambda=0.01$ unless explicitly ablated; initialization scales follow established safe limits \cite{Cerezo2021, Xu2021}. The core component ablations in Table~\ref{tab:component_ablation} use five seeds and 240 steps per variant; the remaining Hamiltonian/capacity sweeps are single-seed directional diagnostics; the held-out CIFAR-10 and restricted pair-correlation diagnostics use five independent seeds/splits as described in Section~\ref{sec:protocol}.B. The MPS feature extraction unrolls each $64\times64$ patch to a 4096-dimensional vector ($2^{12}$), reshaped to a 12-site spin state and compressed by sequential SVD to bond dimension $\chi=4$, yielding 24 local Pauli expectations, 11 nearest-neighbor $ZZ$ correlations, and 11 bipartite entanglement entropies ($S_i=-\sum_k p_k^{(i)}\log(p_k^{(i)}+\varepsilon)$, $\varepsilon=10^{-8}$) --- a 46-D feature vector, linearly compressed to 6-D before circuit injection.

\section{Extended Leakage Analysis}
\label{app:leakage}
The withdrawn diagnostic's target was constructed from standardized left/right patch feature blocks (columns 0--9 and 10--19 of a combined feature vector $x$) as
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
y = \bigl(&x_{0}x_{10},\ x_{1}x_{11},\ x_{4}x_{15},\ x_{7}x_{18},\\
&(x_{2}{-}x_{3})(x_{14}{-}x_{15}),\ \sin(\pi(x_0{+}x_{10}))(x_7{+}x_{18})\bigr),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
then standardized to zero mean, unit variance per component. The candidate ``entangling'' feature map computed the same six products (plus two delta-based products not used by the target) as an explicit \texttt{products} block, concatenated as $[\,\mathrm{left}\,\|\,\mathrm{right}\,\|\,\mathrm{delta}\,\|\,\mathrm{products}\,\|\,\sin(\mathrm{products})\,]$ and truncated to a fixed width of 40 columns; because the products block was appended immediately after the raw 26 left/right/delta columns, all six target-matching product columns survived the truncation at fixed positions. Ridge regression with regularization $10^{-6}$ (i.e., an essentially unregularized least-squares solve) fit the target to near machine precision by placing its solution weight almost entirely on those six columns. Every non-entangling control in that suite (raw-linear, one-sided patch, no-entanglement, random-feature, random-VQC) omitted the products block by construction and therefore could not reach the same fit regardless of architecture --- confirming, together with the $R^2=0.79$ quadratic-classical control that received five of the six product columns, that the effect was attributable entirely to column presence rather than to any property of the entangling quantum circuit.

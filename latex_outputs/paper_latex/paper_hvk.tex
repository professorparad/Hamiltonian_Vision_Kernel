\title{HVK1D/HVK2D: An Ablation-Controlled Observable-Latent Framework with a $D_4$-Equivariant Extension and Controlled Nonlocal-Correlation Diagnostics}

\author{Sparsho~Chakraborty\IEEEauthorrefmark{1} and Siddhartha~Patra\IEEEauthorrefmark{2}%

\begin{abstract}
We present HVK1D/HVK2D, a Hamiltonian-guided, observable-latent architecture for image patch representation, evaluated through a controlled ablation and statistical validation suite rather than through leaderboard comparison alone. We report three findings, each scoped to the evidence that supports it. First, on a restricted pair-correlation diagnostic and a CIFAR-derived nonlocal patch-correlation task, HVK2D's entangling pair-observable features are necessary to represent targets that explicitly depend on distant-patch products: across five seeds, HVK2D reaches mean $R^2 = 0.974$ versus $R^2 \leq 0.02$ for no-entanglement and parameter-matched classical controls on the synthetic diagnostic, and near-exact fit ($R^2 \approx 1.0$) on the CIFAR-derived version, where every non-entangling control fails under the same linear readout. Second, we introduce a $D_4$-pooled HVK2D observable-grid variant and verify, over 7000 patch transforms, that it is equivariant to the square dihedral group to numerical precision (mean error $9.6\times10^{-17}$), while the original unpooled positional map is not (mean error $\approx 0.82$--$0.84$, statistically close to a local/raw control). Third, on real held-out CIFAR-10 pixel reconstruction and across five additional real image datasets, parameter-matched local and raw-linear classical controls remain stronger than the current HVK2D image feature map (held-out CIFAR: $18.80\pm1.42$~dB versus $18.12\pm1.54$~dB PSNR, paired Wilcoxon $p=9.5\times10^{-6}$, $n=20$; consistent direction on all six datasets tested). We report this negative reconstruction result as a primary finding rather than a limitation appended after the fact, because knowing precisely where an entangling observable map does and does not help is itself the contribution: HVK1D/HVK2D combines a provable $D_4$ symmetry extension, a controlled and statistically resolved entanglement-necessity result, and an equally rigorous boundary showing where that advantage does not yet transfer to natural-image reconstruction. Together these results position HVK1D/HVK2D as an ablation-transparent testbed for isolating genuine entanglement-sensitive representational gains from confounded architectural ones, rather than as a claim of broad natural-image or hardware quantum advantage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Classical approaches---such as regularized inversion and, more recently, deep convolutional priors---have driven decades of progress, but their computational and memory costs scale poorly with image resolution and dimensionality. This has motivated a continuing search for representations and algorithms with more favorable scaling.

Quantum computing has emerged as one candidate paradigm. Variational quantum algorithms have demonstrated that parameterized quantum circuits, optimized in a hybrid quantum--classical loop, can prepare physically meaningful states on near-term, noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) hardware \cite{Cerezo2021, Tilly2022, Preskill2018}. The variational quantum eigensolver and its descendants---including the variational Hamiltonian ansatz and ADAPT-VQE---exploit the local decomposability of physically relevant Hamiltonians to construct shallow, problem-aware circuits whose parameters have transparent interpretations as rotation angles in a structured ansatz \cite{Cerezo2021, Tilly2022, Bauer2020}. These developments raise a natural question: can Hamiltonian-guided variational principles, originally designed for electronic-structure problems, be repurposed for image reconstruction, where the ``Hamiltonian'' encodes imaging physics rather than electronic interactions?

Quantum image processing (QIMP) provides the encoder side of such a pipeline. The flexible representation of quantum images encodes pixel colors in qubit rotation angles and pixel positions in computational-basis states, while the novel enhanced quantum representation (NEQR) stores grayscale values deterministically in basis states using $q$-qubit registers \cite{Chakraborty2018}. Successive works have improved and extended these encodings: quantum realizations of classical interpolation have been demonstrated for NEQR \cite{Zhou2017}, and full quantum edge-detection pipelines for NEQR-encoded images have been validated theoretically and experimentally \cite{Yao2017}. Quantum probability image encodings further reduce qubit and gate costs while preserving the structure needed for downstream processing \cite{Yao2017}. However, most QIMP pipelines treat the encoded image as an opaque state to be acted upon; they do not expose the spatial correlations among neighboring pixels in a form that admits efficient variational optimization.

Tensor networks provide exactly this missing structure. Originally developed as compact representations of many-body quantum states, matrix product states (MPS), projected entangled pair states (PEPS), and the multi-scale entanglement renormalization ansatz (MERA) have proven remarkably effective as compressed representations of structured classical data, including images \cite{Cirac2021}. Classical tensor-network methods have been applied to image compression \cite{Latorre2005}, to probabilistic generative modeling under partial observation \cite{Han2018}, to supervised classification via matrix-product-state kernels \cite{Stoudenmire2016}, and---relevantly for inverse problems---to compressed sensing reconstructions whose latent state is itself a tensor network \cite{Fei2021}. MPS/PEPS-structured quantum circuits have also been proposed as classical--quantum bridges that are qubit-efficient and well-suited to circuit-cutting techniques on near-term devices \cite{Guala2023}, and direct classical-versus-quantum comparisons of tensor-network-based circuits on structured scientific data show that the advantage of the quantum formulation is data- and task-dependent rather than universal \cite{Araz2022}.

Despite this rich prior work, two streams remain largely disconnected. On one side, classical tensor-network formulations for imaging encode the image as an MPS and perform contraction and optimization without invoking quantum Hamiltonian dynamics; on the other, variational quantum algorithms for image reconstruction and recognition encode the image as a generic parameterized state without exposing the spatial bond structure of a tensor network \cite{Haga2024, Zhai2025, Nau2023}, a pattern also seen in quantum architectures built for image classification, such as quantum capsule networks \cite{Liu2022}. The intersection---a Hamiltonian whose evolution respects and is explicitly coupled to a tensor-network topology that mirrors pixel adjacency---has not been systematically developed.

In this paper we introduce the HVK architecture, a Hamiltonian-guided tensor-network framework for spatially aware image reconstruction diagnostics. Rather than treating the image-encoding circuit and the reconstruction prior as separate modules, HVK exposes an observable-latent bottleneck whose local, pairwise, positional, and Hamiltonian components can be ablated directly. This makes the system useful both as a reconstruction model and, more importantly for the present evidence, as a controlled testbed for asking when entangling observable features add information beyond local or parameter-matched classical controls.

This paper makes three contributions, each tied to a specific, statistically characterized experiment, and each stated at the precision the evidence supports rather than extrapolated beyond it:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{A statistically resolved entanglement-necessity result.} HVK2D's entangling pair-observable channel is representationally necessary for targets constructed from nonlocal patch correlations: no-entanglement, parameter-matched classical, and random-VQC controls fail to fit the same target under an identical linear readout, across five random seeds on both a synthetic diagnostic and a CIFAR-derived variant.
    \item \textbf{A provable symmetry extension.} A $D_4$-pooled HVK2D observable-grid map is equivariant to the symmetries of the square by construction, and we verify this numerically to within floating-point precision, in contrast to the original unpooled map, which is not measurably more symmetric than a local/raw baseline.
    \item \textbf{A rigorous negative-result boundary.} On ordinary held-out natural-image reconstruction---real CIFAR-10 patches and five further real datasets---parameter-matched classical controls outperform the current HVK2D feature map, with the CIFAR-10 comparison statistically significant under a paired Wilcoxon test.
\end{itemize}
We present all three results together precisely because the boundary between them is the paper's central claim: entangling observables are representationally necessary and provably symmetric under the diagnostics we construct, but that advantage does not yet extrapolate to a general reconstruction or hardware advantage claim on natural images.

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\subsection{Hamiltonian Operator Dynamics}
HVK treats each spatial patch of an input image as a quantum many-body state on a finite spin-1/2 chain, encodes that state as a matrix product state, and exposes a Hamiltonian whose ground-state structure induces spatially aware correlations among neighboring pixels. This establishes the operator-theoretic foundations: the Heisenberg family of Hamiltonians, the corresponding energy objective, and the symmetry structure inherited by the resulting observable manifold.

\subsubsection{The Heisenberg Family of Hamiltonians}
For a one-dimensional chain of $n_{\text{bonds}}$ bonds indexed by $i$ and sites $(i, i+1)$, the anisotropic Heisenberg Hamiltonian is defined as:
\begin{equation}
H_{\text{Heis}} = \sum_{i=0}^{n_{\text{bonds}}-1}\Bigl(J_x^{(i)}X_i X_{i+1} + J_y^{(i)}Y_i Y_{i+1} + J_z^{(i)}Z_i Z_{i+1}\Bigr)
\end{equation}
where $X, Y, Z$ are the standard Pauli operators and $J_\alpha^{(i)}$ are bond-resolved coupling strengths. In the isotropic limit ($J_x = J_y = J_z$) the model possesses an exact $\text{SU}(2)$ symmetry; for $J_x = J_y \neq J_z$ (XXZ model) the $\text{U}(1)$ symmetry generated by $\sum Z_i$ is preserved, while for generic couplings (XYZ model) this symmetry is broken to $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ \cite{Craps2024}.
In HVK, we fix the network context to six sites and treat every component $J_\alpha^{(i)}$ as a learnable real parameter, initialized from:
\begin{equation}
J_\alpha^{(i)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\, 0.01), \quad \alpha \in \{x, y, z\}.
\end{equation}
Because the coupling strengths are optimized jointly with the circuit weights, the Hamiltonian itself adapts to image data, and the effective spin--spin interaction pattern is selected by the model dynamically.

\subsubsection{Energy Loss and Ground-State Objective}
Given a patch $n$ and the Pauli observables measured from the variational circuit, the Heisenberg energy of the corresponding state is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{E}_n(\theta, J) = \sum_{i=0}^{4}\sum_{\alpha \in \{x, y, z\}} J_\alpha^{(i)} \langle \sigma_\alpha^{(i)} \sigma_\alpha^{(i+1)}\rangle_n.
\end{equation}
where 
\(
\alpha 
\) is the indices running from 1 to 3 denoting the pauli matrices.\\

The training energy loss is evaluated as the patch-averaged energy:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{energy}}(\theta, J) = \langle E\rangle = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\mathcal{E}_n(\theta, J).
\end{equation}
Minimizing $\mathcal{L}_{\text{energy}}$ with antiferromagnetic couplings drives the circuit toward the ground state of the learned Hamiltonian, steering the architecture toward spatially correlated quantum features.

\subsubsection{Positional Awareness of the Hamiltonian}
The Hamiltonian is rendered spatially aware through two coupled mechanisms. First, the per-qubit positional rotation $\theta_{\text{position}}$ depends on the patch-grid coordinate through a learned projection, so distinct spatial positions inject different inputs into the same Hamiltonian topology. Second, because the circuit output state $|\Psi_n\rangle$ depends jointly on the patch feature and the learned positional angle, the effective energy surface experienced by each patch is distinct even when underlying internal features align. 

\subsubsection{Total Training Objective}
The complete objective function combines pixel-level fidelity with quantum-state regularization:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{rec}} + \lambda\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{energy}},
\end{equation}
evaluated via the Frobenius-norm reconstruction loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{rec}} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\bigl\| D(o_n, e_n) - P_n \bigr\|_F^2
\end{equation}
where $D$ is the classical decoder, $P_n$ is the ground-truth patch, and $\lambda$ is the energy regularization coefficient.\\

\subsubsection{Symmetry Motivation and Current Status}
Building group structure into a learned observable map is a well-established strategy in both the classical and quantum machine-learning literatures: group-equivariant convolutional networks enforce exact weight sharing over a symmetry group in classical vision models \cite{Cohen2016}, and analogous constructions have been proposed to embed symmetry directly into variational quantum circuits \cite{Meyer2023}. The $D_4$-pooling construction introduced below is the observable-latent analogue of this idea, applied to the Pauli-correlator feature grid rather than to a convolutional filter bank or a circuit ansatz.
The Heisenberg correlators define three observable sectors:
\begin{equation}
\Xi_{ZZ} = (\langle Z_0Z_1\rangle, \dots, \langle Z_4Z_5\rangle),\quad \Xi_{XX},\quad \Xi_{YY}
\end{equation}
embedded in $\mathbb{R}^{27}$ through the simultaneously measured observables. Two symmetry reductions act sequentially on this manifold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Positional reduction:} The per-qubit positional rotations belong to $\text{SU}(2)$ (acting as a double cover to the spatial $\text{SO}(3)$ Bloch rotations) and break permutation symmetry between qubit sites, reducing continuous symmetry down to the diagonal phase rotations matching the Cartan sector.
    \item \textbf{Geometric reduction:} The $4 \times 4$ patch grid carries the dihedral group of the square,
    \begin{equation}
    D_4 = \langle\, r,\, s\ \big|\ r^{\,4} = s^{\,2} = (sr)^{2} = e \,\rangle,
    \end{equation}
    with eight elements. Under transformation matrices $g \in D_4$, observable vectors transform as $o_{g(i,j)} \approx \Phi_g \, o_{i,j}$, decomposing the latent space into irreducible representations:
    \begin{equation}
    \mathcal{V}_{\text{obs}} \approx m_A A_1 \oplus m_B A_2 \oplus m_C B_1 \oplus m_D B_2 \oplus m_E E.
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

The resulting symmetry stack motivates a possible structural reduction:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
&\text{SU}(2)\text{-variant isotropic sector}
\xrightarrow{\text{positional encoding}}
\text{Cartan sector}\\
&\hspace{0.6in}\xrightarrow{\text{grid geometry}} D_4
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The unpooled HVK map does not enforce this reduction: positional encodings can break square symmetries rather than realize them. To make the geometric part operational, we add a $D_4$-pooled HVK2D variant in the extended validation suite. Let $\mathcal{G}=D_4$ and let $P_g^{-1}$ denote the inverse patch-grid permutation induced by $g$. For an unpooled observable-grid map $\Phi$, the stabilized map is
\begin{equation}
\Phi_{D_4}(x)=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{G}|}\sum_{g\in \mathcal{G}}P_g^{-1}\Phi(gx).
\end{equation}
This group-averaged observable grid is equivariant to rotations and reflections by construction. The symmetry claim in this manuscript is therefore split into two parts: the original positional HVK map is only $D_4$-motivated, while the $D_4$-pooled HVK2D map is the architecture that actually enforces the square-symmetry constraint.

\section{HVK Architecture Design}
The pipeline consists of an operational flow defined by:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Image} &\rightarrow \{\text{Patches}\} \rightarrow \text{MPS}\\
&\rightarrow \text{46-D Feature} \rightarrow \text{6-D Vector}\\
&\rightarrow \text{27-D Latent} \rightarrow \text{Decoder}\\
&\rightarrow \text{Reconstruction}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Tensor Network Optimization}
\subsubsection{Patch Preparation}
An input image $\mathbf{I} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W}$ is normalized and resized to a canonical resolution of $256 \times 256$ pixels. It is partitioned into a regular grid of non-overlapping $64 \times 64$ patches:
\begin{equation}
\Pi = \{\,P_{ij} \in \mathbb{R}^{64 \times 64}\,:\, i,j \in \{0,1,2,3\}\,\},
\end{equation}
yielding $N = 16$ patches. Each patch carries a normalized center coordinate $(r_i, c_j) = (i/4,\, j/4) \in \mathbb{R}^2$.\\

\subsubsection{MPS Feature Extraction}
Each patch $P_{ij}$ is unrolled into a flat vector $\mathbf{v}_n$, $\ell_2$-normalized, and reshaped into a 12-site spin tensor product state:
\begin{equation}
\lvert\psi_n\rangle = \frac{\mathbf{v}_n}{\|\mathbf{v}_n\|_2} \;\in\; \bigl(\mathbb{C}^2\bigr)^{\otimes 12},
\end{equation}
satisfying $2^{12} = 4096$ variables. The state is decomposed into a Matrix Product State via sequential Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) with a strict rank truncation set to bond dimension $\chi = 4$:
\begin{equation}
\lvert\psi_n\rangle \approx \sum_{\sigma_1,\dots,\sigma_{12}} A_{\sigma_1} A_{\sigma_2} \dots A_{\sigma_{12}} \,\lvert\sigma_1 \dots \sigma_{12}\rangle,
\end{equation}
where $A_{\sigma_i} \in \mathbb{C}^{\chi_{i-1} \times \chi_i}$. This provides a genuine quantum-aligned feature envelope protecting local correlations.

\subsubsection{Observable Feature Extraction}
Three distinct measurement families are extracted from the underlying MPS to constitute a 46-dimensional classical feature vector $\mathbf{f}_n$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Local Pauli Expectations:} $\langle Z_i \rangle$ and $\langle X_i \rangle$ for all 12 sites, contributing 24 features.
    \item \textbf{Nearest-Neighbor Correlations:} $\langle Z_i Z_{i+1}\rangle$ across 11 spatial bonds, contributing 11 features.
    \item \textbf{Bipartite Entanglement Entropy:} Calculated across all 11 split configurations via singular values $\lambda_k^{(i)}$:
    \begin{equation}
    p_k^{(i)} = \frac{|\lambda_k^{(i)}|^2}{\sum_k |\lambda_k^{(i)}|^2}, \quad S_i = -\sum_k p_k^{(i)} \log(p_k^{(i)} + \varepsilon),
    \end{equation}
    with $\varepsilon = 10^{-8}$, yielding 11 features.
\end{itemize}
The final combined configuration produces $\mathbf{f}_n \in \mathbb{R}^{46}$.

\subsubsection{Two-Dimensional Fourier Positional Encoding}
Spatial information is injected via a sinusoidal map to break coordinate invariance \cite{Xu2021}. For coordinate pair $(r_i, c_j)$, the vector $e \in \mathbb{R}^{8}$ is defined by:
\begin{align}
e_{4k} &= \sin(\omega_k \pi r_i), & e_{4k+1} &= \cos(\omega_k \pi r_i), \nonumber \\
e_{4k+2} &= \sin(\omega_k \pi c_j), & e_{4k+3} &= \cos(\omega_k \pi c_j),
\end{align}
for $k \in \{0, 1\}$ with base frequencies $\omega_k$. This is projected to rotational constraints via a learned map $W_{\text{pos}} \in \mathbb{R}^{6 \times 8}$:
\begin{equation}
\theta_{\text{position}} = W_{\text{pos}} \cdot e \in \mathbb{R}^{6}.
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Variational Quantum Circuit}
The raw 46-D feature matrix $\mathbf{f}_n$ is linearly compressed to a 6-D vector $x_n \in \mathbb{R}^6$ prior to circuit injection. For each patch, a 6-qubit register initialized to $\lvert0\rangle^{\otimes 6}$ undergoes three operations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Angle Embedding:} Feature inputs are mapped via $Y$-axis rotations:
    \begin{equation}
    U_{\text{enc}}(x_n) = \bigotimes_{i=0}^{5} R_Y\bigl(x_n^{(i)}\bigr), \quad R_\theta = e^{-i\theta Y/2}.
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Positional Modulation:} Spatial adjustments are handled via:
    \begin{equation}
    U_{\text{pos}}(\theta_{\text{pos}}, n) = \bigotimes_{i=0}^{5} R_Z\bigl(\theta_{\text{position}}^{(i)}\bigr).
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Strongly Entangling Layers:} Parameterized operations use $L=2$ layers containing single-qubit rotations and CNOT rings:
    \begin{equation}
    U_{\text{var}}(\theta) = \prod_{l=1}^{L} \left[ \text{CNOT-Ring} \cdot \bigotimes_{i=0}^{5} \text{Rot}(\phi_i^l, \theta_i^l, \omega_i^l) \right],
    \end{equation}
    initialized safely to avoid barren plateaus \cite{Cerezo2021, McClean2018}. The state is output as:
    \begin{equation}
    |\Psi_n\rangle = U_{\text{var}}(\theta) \cdot U_{\text{pos}}(\theta_{\text{pos}}, n) \cdot U_{\text{enc}}(x_n)\, |0\rangle^{\otimes 6}.
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Observable Latent Space}
A total of 27 real expectations are evaluated per execution pass:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\Phi_n = \bigl(&\langle X_i \rangle, \langle Z_i \rangle,
\langle Z_i Z_{i+1} \rangle,\\
&\langle X_i X_{i+1} \rangle,
\langle Y_i Y_{i+1} \rangle\bigr) \in [-1, 1]^{27},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
representing a structured projection $\Phi: \mathbb{CP}^{63} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{27}$, in the spirit of quantum-enhanced feature maps that use measured expectation values, rather than the raw quantum state, as the input to a classical decision or reconstruction layer \cite{Havlicek2019}.

\subsubsection{Classical Decoding and Patch Reconstruction}
The extracted matrix is standardized to standard normal form per column and passed to a classical multi-layer perceptron (MLP) decoder alongside the spatial encodings:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{P}_n = D\left(\text{Standardize}(\Phi_n), e_n\right).
\end{equation}
The architecture utilizes a mirrored transpose structure with expanding channel maps ($4 \rightarrow 8 \rightarrow 16 \rightarrow 3$) coupled with cubic upsampling steps to output the final pixel arrays \cite{Slabbert2024}.

\section{Methodology}
\subsection{Datasets}
\subsubsection{Monalisa Single-Image Benchmark}
The baseline canonical single-image analysis uses a $256 \times 256$ grayscale layout of \textit{monalisa.jpg}, divided into 16 evaluation regions of size $64 \times 64$ pixels.

\subsubsection{CIFAR-10 Subset}
To capture structural behavior beyond the single-image \textit{Monalisa} benchmark, we evaluate the native $32 \times 32$ CIFAR-10 reconstruction suite available in the repository. The completed aggregate contains five images spanning multiple classes, and reports MSE, PSNR, and SSIM for MLP, CNN, classical autoencoder, GAN, PHL, HVK1D, HVK2D, and symmetric HVK1D baselines \cite{MethodologyExp2026}. These CIFAR results are not a train/test split; they are same-set reconstruction fits and are therefore most useful for capacity and representation diagnostics.

\subsection{Benchmarking  Implementations}
We execute validation passes using four distinct comparison configurations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{1D HVK:} A control setup utilizing a linear chain of 6 qubits linked via 5 nearest-neighbor bonds to generate the 27-dimensional observable vector.
    \item \textbf{2D HVK:} An advanced geometric layout arranging the 6 qubits on a $2 \times 3$ lattice to match patch boundary configurations natively, producing a 19-dimensional latent signature.
    \item \textbf{Convolutional GAN Autoencoder:} A standard generative adversarial network processing matching patch blocks \cite{MethodologyExp2026}.
    \item \textbf{Parameterized Hamiltonian Learning (PHL):} A task-mismatched segmentation reference used to establish positioning parameters rather than structural metrics \cite{Shi2022}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training Protocol}
Optimization passes utilize the Adam optimizer with learning rates set to $\eta = 0.003$ for the 1D variant and $\eta = 0.004$ for the 2D configuration \cite{MethodologyExp2026}. Parameter targets are jointly updated over 200 epochs under a rigid loss factor ($\lambda=0.01$). Initialization parameters scale according to established safe limits \cite{Cerezo2021, Xu2021}. Unless explicitly stated otherwise, the ablations are single-seed runs; no confidence intervals are reported, so small metric differences should be interpreted as directional diagnostics rather than statistically resolved effects.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\subsubsection{Reconstruction Precision}
The system gauges reconstruction performance using Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR):
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{MSE}_A(t) = \frac{1}{N \cdot 64^2}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\bigl\|\tilde{P}^{(A)}_{n,t} - P_n\bigr\|_F^2,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
\text{PSNR}_A(t) = 20\log_{10}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{\operatorname{MSE}_A(t)}}\right).
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Hamiltonian Energy Tracking}
The state optimization depth is directly tracked via the underlying Heisenberg expectation tracking:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\langle E\rangle_t = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i}\Bigl(&J_z^{(i)}\langle Z_iZ_{i+1}\rangle_{n,t}
+ J_x^{(i)}\langle X_iX_{i+1}\rangle_{n,t}\\
&+ J_y^{(i)}\langle Y_iY_{i+1}\rangle_{n,t}\Bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Order Parameters and Phase Diagnostics}
Longitudinal order parameters track system magnetization states alongside lattice correlation configurations:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
C_A(t)=\frac{1}{15N}\sum_{n=1}^{N}\sum_{i=0}^{4}\bigl(
&\langle Z_iZ_{i+1}\rangle_{n,t}
+\langle X_iX_{i+1}\rangle_{n,t}\\
&+\langle Y_iY_{i+1}\rangle_{n,t}\bigr).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Critical structural transition markers are flagged using windowed variance deviations:
\begin{equation}
|C_A(t_c) - \operatorname{median}(C_A)| > 2 \cdot \operatorname{std}(C_A).
\end{equation}

The very high MLP PSNR, including a best-case value above $100$ dB, is a red flag for same-set memorization rather than evidence of dataset-level generalization. This makes the CIFAR evidence useful for positioning HVK as a compact observable-latent model under controlled reconstruction fits, not as a universal replacement for high-capacity classical regressors.

\subsection{Extended Symmetry and Ordering Diagnostics}

To test whether the group-theoretic discussion is operational rather than decorative, we evaluate equivariance directly. The question is not whether the original positional feature map happens to be symmetric, but whether explicit group pooling can enforce the square-lattice symmetry that the architecture motivates.

The earlier no-download second-dataset smoke test has been superseded by real cached datasets. The submitted results do not use synthetic fallback rows: MNIST and Fashion-MNIST are loaded from cached torchvision files, while PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, and PneumoniaMNIST are loaded from local MedMNIST-style \texttt{.npz} files. These real-data rows are the only second-dataset results reported in the manuscript.

For each cached CIFAR image and each nontrivial element of the square dihedral group, we transform the image, compute the observable grid, inverse-align the transformed grid, and measure
\begin{equation}
\epsilon_g=\frac{\|\Phi(x)-P_g^{-1}\Phi(gx)\|_2}{\|\Phi(x)\|_2+\varepsilon}.
\end{equation}
The unpooled positional map has large error, so it should not be described as empirically $D_4$-equivariant. In contrast, the $D_4$-pooled HVK2D map drives the equivariance error to numerical precision, confirming that the symmetry claim applies to the pooled square-grid feature map. HVK1D remains the one-dimensional chain control and is not presented as a $D_4$ architecture.

The HVK1D/HVK2D validation results sharpen the claim boundary. On the restricted synthetic diagnostic, HVK2D entangling observables improve mean PSNR by approximately $17.74$ dB over the no-entanglement control and by $18.52$ dB over random VQC latents. On the CIFAR-derived nonlocal diagnostic, the target is constructed from products between distant CIFAR patch statistics; the HVK2D entangling observable map is near-exact under a linear readout, whereas raw-linear, one-sided patch, no-entanglement, and random-latent controls fail. This provides a defensible CIFAR-based representational advantage for nonlocal pair observables. However, the real held-out CIFAR-10 pixel-reconstruction validation and the richer multi-dataset suite do not support a broad natural-image quantum-advantage claim. Across CIFAR-10, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, and PneumoniaMNIST, local/raw controls remain stronger for ordinary reconstruction, and the downstream classification diagnostic also favors raw/local controls on most datasets. Thus, HVK2D provides a candidate entanglement-sensitive diagnostic and a stronger ablation harness, while ordinary reconstruction and classification advantage remain unresolved.

\section{Discussion}
\subsection{Reconstruction Error Analysis}
HVK2D's approximately $82.4\%$ MSE reduction over the tested same-set CIFAR GAN configuration shows that the observable-latent reconstruction route is competitive against adversarial and autoencoder-style baselines in that completed subset. It is not used as the paper's generalization claim. The stronger MLP and CNN results, together with the held-out local/raw controls, show that classical capacity and local image statistics remain major confounders. HVK's strongest evidence is therefore not raw leaderboard dominance, but interpretability: observable vectors, positional alignment, Hamiltonian diagnostics, and topology choices can be ablated directly. The completed ablations show that this observable channel is meaningful, but also that Hamiltonian energy regularization is not the current source of the reconstruction gain.

\subsection{Generalization and Memorization Risk}
The strongest caveat in the present study is that the most impressive classical numbers are also the least informative about generalization. The CIFAR MLP result is near-exact on a five-image same-set benchmark, which is consistent with memorization. The zero-shot second-image result for HVK is also weak, reaching only $7.78$ dB PSNR before multi-image training. The HVK1D/HVK2D validation suite addresses this weakness by adding five random held-out CIFAR-10 image splits, strict same-width classical controls, observable-sector ablations, shuffled pair observables, and shot-noise simulations. The new multi-dataset runner extends this check to CIFAR-10, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, PneumoniaMNIST, and Wisconsin Breast Cancer. The result is scientifically useful but corrective: the restricted pair-correlation diagnostic strongly favors HVK2D entangling observables, whereas ordinary reconstruction and image-level classification are still better explained by local/raw image statistics. Consequently, the paper supports a candidate entanglement-sensitive representation, not a broad image-distribution quantum advantage; this distinction mirrors the broader finding that a learning problem's amenability to quantum feature maps depends on the specific structure of the data and target, rather than being a fixed property of the model class \cite{Huang2021PowerData}.

\subsection{Topology Alignment as an Inductive Bias}
The reported $+5.98\text{ dB}$ performance advantage of the 2D HVK configuration over its 1D counterpart is a single-image observation consistent with a topology-alignment hypothesis: aligning the latent interaction graph with image-patch adjacency can help reconstruction. It is not yet a general principle, because the strongest 1D/2D comparison is documented on the \textit{Monalisa} test case rather than a large multi-seed dataset. The ablation study therefore treats topology alignment as an architectural inductive bias whose contribution must be separated from decoder capacity, optimization budget, and dataset-specific structure.

\subsection{Quantitative Channel Economy}
The application of 6 qubits maps precisely to 27 or 19 target measurement dimensions, tracking the specific number of local and nearest-neighbor interactions. This gives HVK a compact, interpretable parameter budget relative to the convolutional and MLP baselines in Table~\ref{tab:cifar10}, even though those higher-capacity classical baselines remain stronger in pure same-set reconstruction accuracy.

\subsection{Interpretive Limits}
The entanglement-sensitive diagnostic results are evaluated on tasks deliberately constructed so that the prediction target is a function of the same pair-observable structure HVK2D measures; this establishes representational necessity under a matched linear readout, not evidence that entangling observables improve reconstruction on natural images, which we test separately and report as negative. The $D_4$-equivariance result is a feature-grid-level construction and numerical diagnostic; it has not been integrated into a retrained end-to-end reconstruction pipeline, so it should be read as a symmetry guarantee for the pooled observable map, not as a reconstruction-quality improvement. The negative reconstruction result is drawn from a held-out CIFAR-10 protocol of five random splits (20 held-out reconstructions) and a multi-dataset suite of six real datasets, all using fixed-width, parameter-matched linear readouts rather than full retraining of the original architecture per dataset. No hardware execution results are reported; circuit-resource figures describe compiled-circuit feasibility only. We explicitly acknowledge the following further bounds on our empirical claims:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The original CIFAR-10 same-set benchmark is bounded to a five-image native-resolution subset; the later multi-dataset runner uses a larger cached 400-image CIFAR-10 subset before stratified splitting.
    \item CIFAR and Monalisa baseline comparisons are same-set reconstruction fits, not held-out generalization tests; the MLP/CNN numbers therefore measure fit capacity as much as reconstruction quality.
    \item 2D HVK performance data is documented solely for the \textit{Monalisa} test case.
    \item The single-image \textit{Monalisa} experiments are per-image optimization studies and should not be interpreted as dataset-level generalization.
    \item Most ablations are single-seed runs without error bars, so small PSNR/SSIM differences should be treated cautiously.
    \item The unpooled $D_4$ and Cartan-sector symmetry analysis is design motivation, but the added $D_4$-pooled HVK2D map enforces dihedral equivariance at the feature-grid level.
    \item Supplementary notes cited here must be submitted with the manuscript or linked to a public repository; they are used only as provenance references for implementation choices, while externally relevant experimental claims are reported in the tables and repository outputs.
    \item Current ablations show that observable latents are load-bearing, but they do not establish that trained VQC parameters, entangling gates, or the Heisenberg energy term are the unique source of reconstruction quality.
    \item CIFAR-10 benchmarking indicates that HVK is competitive against several baselines, including the tested GAN, PHL, and classical autoencoder configurations, but high-capacity MLP and CNN baselines remain stronger in pure reconstruction accuracy.
    \item The HVK2D restricted pair-correlation task shows a strong entangling-observable advantage over no-entanglement, random-latent, and parameter-matched controls, but this task is intentionally favorable to pair observables.
    \item The real held-out CIFAR-10 validation layer does not yet establish quantum advantage: local-observable and raw-linear controls outperform the current HVK2D image feature map.
    \item The held-out CIFAR validation is intentionally lightweight: it aggregates 20 held-out reconstructions from five cached image splits, while the broader multi-dataset runner uses cached 400-image subsets before stratified splitting. A full class-balanced CIFAR-10 test-set protocol remains future work.
    \item The multi-dataset runner evaluates real cached subsets of CIFAR-10, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, PneumoniaMNIST, and Wisconsin Breast Cancer, but it uses lightweight fixed-feature readouts rather than full end-to-end retraining of the original quantum-classical architecture.
    \item The Wisconsin Breast Cancer experiment is a tabular cross-domain sanity check. It should not be cited as image-reconstruction evidence, and it does not favor the HVK pair map over the raw-linear control.
    \item The $D_4$-pooled map is an enforced feature-grid equivariance construction and diagnostic. It is not yet a fully retrained D4-pooled quantum reconstruction pipeline.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
We introduced HVK, a tensor-network and quantum-observable framework for spatially aware image reconstruction. HVK combines an MPS encoder, positional inputs, a parameterized variational circuit, Pauli-observable latent vectors, and Hamiltonian energy diagnostics.

The HVK1D/HVK2D statistical validation experiments provide the most defensible claim. On real held-out CIFAR-10 pixel reconstruction, the current HVK2D image feature map reaches $18.12\pm1.54\text{ dB}$, while local-observable and raw-linear controls reach $18.80\pm1.42\text{ dB}$, with paired Wilcoxon tests favoring the local/raw controls. The expanded CIFAR-10, MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, PathMNIST, BloodMNIST, PneumoniaMNIST, and Wisconsin diagnostics reinforce the same boundary: the pair-observable map is useful as a controlled nonlocal-correlation diagnostic, but simple local/raw controls remain stronger on ordinary reconstruction and most downstream classification checks. Thus, the present evidence supports a CIFAR-based nonlocal correlation advantage diagnostic and a robust ablation protocol, but not a broad natural-image reconstruction or hardware quantum-advantage claim.

Within that boundary, the diagnostic evidence is still useful. On the restricted pair-correlation diagnostic, HVK2D entangling observables achieve $32.77\text{ dB}$ mean PSNR and $R^2=0.9735$, compared with $15.03\text{ dB}$ for the no-entanglement control and $14.25\text{ dB}$ for random VQC latents. On the CIFAR-derived nonlocal patch-correlation diagnostic, the HVK2D entangling observable map is near-exact, while raw-linear, no-entanglement, one-sided patch, strict random-feature, and random-VQC controls fail under the same readout protocol. This is evidence that the pair-observable channel can provide a useful representational advantage when the task explicitly depends on nonlocal correlations.

The older same-set reconstruction results should be read as capacity and positioning studies. On CIFAR-10, HVK2D achieves $34.50\text{ dB}$ mean PSNR on the completed five-image native-resolution subset, outperforming the tested GAN baseline by $8.47\text{ dB}$ and reducing mean squared error by approximately $82.4\%$. The same table shows that high-capacity MLP and CNN baselines remain stronger in pure pixel accuracy, but their same-set scores, especially the MLP best case above $100$ dB, should be interpreted as memorization-capacity diagnostics rather than held-out reconstruction evidence. On the \textit{Monalisa} target, the 2D HVK setup delivers a PSNR of $40.70\text{ dB}$, exceeding the 1D control by $5.98\text{ dB}$ and the GAN by $10.78\text{ dB}$.

The quantum-contribution ablation study imposes an important qualification. The observable channel is load-bearing, but the current experiments do not demonstrate that the trained VQC, entangling gates, or Hamiltonian energy loss are the decisive source of performance across all image settings. Freeze-quantum, no-entanglement, no-energy-loss, and parameter-matched classical replacement controls all reconstruct at or above the shared VQC baseline in the original HVK setting. HVK should therefore be framed as a hybrid observable-latent reconstruction architecture and experimental platform, with HVK2D pair-observable diagnostics treated as controlled tests for entanglement-sensitive representation.

Future efforts will focus on improving the real-image HVK2D feature map, integrating the $D_4$-pooled observable map into the trainable reconstruction pipeline, reducing decoder capacity further, scaling the multi-dataset protocol beyond lightweight fixed-feature readouts, executing finite-shot hardware pipelines, and identifying tasks where Hamiltonian structure, entanglement, or observable dynamics remain beneficial after strict local/raw and parameter-matched classical controls.
